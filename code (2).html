Quiet Quitting in School: Understanding Disengagement

Explore why students might "quiet quit" and how to address it.

What is Quiet Quitting in School?

Quiet quitting refers to students who attend classes but do the bare minimum. They're present but not engaged, often due to burnout, lack of interest, or systemic issues.

Image: A student appearing disengaged in a classroom (source: Unsplash).

Causes of Quiet Quitting

  • Overwhelming workload or stress from exams.
  • Lack of motivation or relevance in subjects.
  • Personal issues like mental health or family problems.
  • Perception that effort doesn't lead to rewards.

Image: A student overwhelmed by schoolwork (source: Unsplash).

Effects on Students and Schools

It can lead to lower grades, missed opportunities, and a cycle of disengagement. Schools may see higher dropout rates or reduced participation.

Image: An empty classroom symbolizing disengagement (source: Unsplash).

Tips to Combat Quiet Quitting

  • Encourage open communication between students and teachers.
  • Promote work-life balance and mental health support.
  • Make learning engaging with interactive methods.
  • Set realistic goals and celebrate small wins.
